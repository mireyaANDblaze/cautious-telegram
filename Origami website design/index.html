Origami is an ancient art form that is believed to have been around for nearly 2000 years, derived from the Japanese tradition of paper-folding

Facts About Origami:

    Origami is derived from two Japanese words: Ori (folded) and Kami (paper).
    The first book ever published about Origami was entitled “Sembazuru Orikata”(Thousand Crane Folding)
    As a good luck token for all the future dangers to be faced, Samurais commonly used to present gifts of origami to each other



1. Pigeon Origami

Interesting facts bout Pigeons:

  • Pigeons are very complex and intelligent animals. They are one of a small number of species to pass the ‘mirror test’ (a test of self recognition)
  • They can also recognise each letter of the human alphabet and differentiate between photographs.
  • Pigeons are highly sociable animals. They are often seen in flocks of 20-30 birds.

3. Cicada Origami

Interesting facts bout Cicadas:

  • The body of a cicada is similar to that of a violin or a guitar.
  • Cicadas make noise like toy frogs, especially male Cicadas
  • Most have red-orange eyes. But occasionally cicadas have blue, white or grayish eyes.

4. Camel Origami

Interesting facts bout camels:

  • The word ‘camel’ comes from an Arabic word, when it’s literally translated, it means beauty
  • During the Second World War, German tank drivers would drive their vehicles over camel droppings, thinking it would bring them good luck.
  • There are more than 14 million camels in the world – most of them live in the Middle East, Asia and Australia.

5. Panda Origami

Interesting facts bout Pandas:

  • Giant pandas are good at climbing trees and can also swim.
  • Pandas need at least 2 different bamboo species in their range to avoid starvation
  • A 45-kilo adult panda spends as long as 14 hours eating, and it can eat 12 to 38 kilos of bamboo a day.

Steps to make a panda Origami:
  1. Fold and unfold the paper in half both ways.
  2. Fold both sides in along the dotted lines.
  3. Fold and unfold the bottom and top along the dotted lines.
  4. Fold the paper behind along the dotted line.
  5. Turn the paper over.
  6. Fold the top of the paper down along the dotted line.
  7. Fold the paper up along the dotted line.
  8. Fold and unfold the paper in half and leave slightly folded to give a 3D look.
